Ypsilanti — "Ypsi" to locals — is a city of historic neighborhoods, tree-lined streets, and a strong community identity. From the well-preserved homes of Normal Park to the shops and restaurants of Depot Town, mature trees are part of what defines Ypsilanti's character.

Foundations Tree Experts serves Ypsilanti and Ypsilanti Township with the same crew and standards we bring to every job across Southeast Michigan. We're just minutes away and regularly work in the area.

## Neighborhoods we serve in Ypsilanti

We work throughout Ypsilanti and Ypsilanti Township, including:

- **Normal Park** — west of Main Street, south of EMU campus, meticulously maintained historic homes with mature street trees
- **Depot Town** — historic district east of downtown along the Huron River
- **College Heights** — diverse neighborhood in the heart of Ypsi, walking distance to downtown and EMU
- **Riverside** — scenic neighborhood along the Huron River corridor
- **Midtown** — historic homes and tree-lined streets
- In Ypsilanti Township: **Greene Farms**, **Lincoln Pines**, **Partridge Creek**

## Tree challenges in Ypsilanti

Ypsilanti's older housing stock and established tree canopy create specific needs:

- **Emerald ash borer** — the same regional devastation that hit Ann Arbor has impacted Ypsilanti's ash tree population. Dead and declining ash trees near homes and streets need safe removal.
- **Aging trees on older lots** — many of Ypsilanti's historic neighborhoods have trees that were planted when the homes were built decades ago. These mature trees can outgrow their lots, encroaching on rooflines, power lines, and foundations.
- **Urban environment stress** — limited root space, soil compaction, and utility conflicts challenge tree health in denser neighborhoods.
- **Huron River corridor** — properties near the river often have large cottonwoods, willows, and other riparian species that require specialized care.

## Ypsilanti's growing urban forest

The City of Ypsilanti recently received a $143,703 state grant to complete its first comprehensive public tree inventory, develop an urban forest management plan, plant new street trees, and train staff and volunteers. This investment reflects the city's commitment to maintaining and growing its tree canopy — and means homeowners should be aware of evolving tree care standards and regulations in the area.
